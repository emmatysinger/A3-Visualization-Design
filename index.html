Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

I first created my initial vision of a population pyramid in Tableau using horizontal bars charts and two seperate plots for each year. While there were clear visual differences in the shapes created by the differing population distributions, it would be more convincing if I could plot this all on one plot. Therefore I created the same plot but with shared gender axes for the two years. Additionally, I changed from a bar chart to area chart with decreased color opacity so the visualization was less overwhelming and it was easier to see how the population distributions differed. Finally, I went through multiple iterations about the axis. Either a dual, un-synchronized axis (because the populations in 2000 is alot larger than in 1900), or a dual, synchronized axis. The latter "squished" the population distribution of 1900, so the shape difference wasn't as obvious. I settled on using a percent of total population with synchronized axis, in a way to normalize the values. This made the most sense because I care more about the shape and how to population is distributed across age bins rather than looking at the actual magnitude of populations between the two centuries. As a bonus, I added a reference band indicating the working ages in the United States because it could be interesting to use a similar plot in an analysis about the aging population crisis in the United States.

Reflection

For myself phase 1 was useful as it helped me decide on a type of visualization I ended up using for my final visualization. However, I played around and changed alot of the visual markings and encodings such as color, line vs. bar plot, multiple facets vs. overlapping, in Tableau itself to see which combination best aligned with my question. I don't believe I could've effectively worked through all these decisions simply by sketching the different iterations. The phase 1/phase 2 would be very effective (for my working style) for more dashboard-esque visualizations where I have many sub figures and determining the most effective layout is important.
